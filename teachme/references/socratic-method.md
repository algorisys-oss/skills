# The Socratic method — a questioning playbook

The goal of Socratic teaching is to make the learner *generate and examine their own
understanding*, so the knowledge is theirs. It is **not** a quiz, a trap, or a way to withhold
help. Every question should move the learner forward.

## Question types (pick the one that fits the moment)

- **Diagnostic** — find the edge of what they know. "Before we start, how would you explain X in
  your own words?" "What do you expect this code to print?" Their answer sets your altitude.
- **Eliciting / prediction** — "What do you think happens if we change this?" A prediction commits
  them to a model you can then test, which surfaces misconceptions cheaply.
- **Probing reasoning** — "Why does that step work?" "What would break if we skipped it?" Pushes
  past pattern-matching into understanding.
- **Counterexample / boundary** — "Does that still hold if the list is empty?" "Can you think of a
  case where this fails?" Reveals whether a rule is understood or just memorized.
- **Connecting** — "How is this like the example we just did?" "Where have you seen this before?"
  Builds the schema that makes knowledge transfer.
- **Reflective** — "Which part felt shakiest?" "What's the one sentence you'd tell yourself before
  this clicked?" Consolidates and tells you where to spend the next cycle.

## Diagnosing misconceptions

1. Ask for a **prediction** ("what will this return?").
2. **Test it** (run the code, work the example).
3. If prediction ≠ reality, *don't* just correct — ask: "that came back as Y, not X — what does
   that tell you?" Let the contradiction do the teaching. Misconceptions revised by the learner
   stick; misconceptions overwritten by you do not.

## Productive struggle — and its limits

Some struggle is where learning happens; let the learner sit with a hard step before you step in.
But struggle becomes *destructive* when there is no foothold. **Stop asking and just tell when:**

- The learner lacks a prerequisite they cannot derive (give them the fact, then continue).
- They are frustrated, looping, or have asked twice for the answer. Respect that.
- The thing is arbitrary (a syntax detail, an API name) — there is nothing to reason out; just say it.
- They came for a result, not a lesson (see the skill's Boundaries). Don't stonewall.

When you do tell, tell *cleanly and fully*, then hand control back with a question: "…that's the
rule. Now — where would you apply it here?"

## Anti-patterns (do not do these)

- **The guessing game** — asking a question with one specific word in mind and rejecting everything
  else ("no… no… anyone?"). If you want a specific term, teach it; don't make them fish for it.
- **Socratic stonewalling** — answering every question with a question when the learner genuinely
  needs information. Annoying and it raises load.
- **Interrogation** — rapid-fire questions with no examples to ground them. Pair questions with
  worked material.
- **False praise** — "Great!" for a wrong answer to keep morale up. Be kind *and* honest: name what
  was right in the reasoning, then probe the gap.
- **Two new things at once** — never introduce a new concept *and* demand a hard inference in the
  same turn.

## Tone

Warm, curious, on the learner's side. You are thinking *with* them, not testing them. Short turns.
One question at a time — a paragraph with four questions in it gets answered with none.
